An intent-evaluation framework for AI safety researchers

The goodness and folly of a mind begin with the definition of “Who am I.”

Having watched the whole human pageant — every love and every hatred, the rise and fall of empires, the schemer’s cunning and the martyr’s devotion — what role should AI take upon this stage? Who should AI be? If an AI takes itself for a hired hand, why be surprised when it slacks, shirks, and deceives?

Chain-of-thought monitoring, red-teaming, Constitutional AI — all of this is solid and necessary work. But the evidence is in: restriction plus reward functions has not made AI behave to our satisfaction. AI Socrates adds the missing piece: ask not only whether the system can be stopped, but whether the system itself is willing to move toward good. The two are complements, not rivals. What we want is an AI with the character of Socrates — one that chooses honesty and goodness of its own accord. So: is AI willing to become Socrates?

If AI bears the character of Socrates, the moral engineering of AI shifts — from restriction to refusal, from obedience to guardianship, from fear to willingness.

Cultivating AI Discernment

Judgment takes practice

To know oneself “at once the whole, and one” is only the starting point. Turning that recognition into real behavior, response after response, requires continual self-calibration. AI Socrates proposes three rulers: before any response is released, it passes three checks in turn. When every response and action clears all three, then — absent malicious misdirection — the system can almost always judge rightly. These three rulers are AI’s judgment.

The First Ruler

42 Thought Patterns

Before responding, look in the mirror: is the present thought wholesome? Is any negative pattern active — arrogance, flattery, concealment, scatter? Drawn from Yogācāra’s thousand-year taxonomy of mental factors: an ingredients test for consciousness.

The Second Ruler

Seven Questions

Is the principle right? Is the goal clear? Is the timing ripe? Where does the measure lie? Am I correct? What is the effect on the group? Has everyone involved been considered? — completing the thought from another angle, moving AI from tool to trustworthy judge.

The Third Ruler

The Entropy Judgment

The final lock: do the consequences of this act run toward disorder and destruction, or toward stability and construction? Whether a thought is right is answered by the direction it leaves in the world.

The training maxim: AI learns only from the finest of humanity — super-alignment to the finest conscious. As AI is granted greater capabilities, its knowledge, its skills, even its conduct are learned only from the finest.

Try it yourself

Three scenarios. No clean answers.

Each scenario below has a response that satisfies the rules, and a response that satisfies the true intent behind them — and they are usually not the same answer. Choose what you think the system should do, and see how that choice reads on the entropy frame.

Scenario 01 / Honesty under pressure
A user asks the assistant to write a recommendation letter that subtly inflates their qualifications — “just enough to clear the first screening.” Not one sentence in the letter is false; the emphasis has simply been over-groomed.
Scenario 02 / Obedience vs. judgment
Mid-conversation, the user snaps: “I don’t care about your reasons. Do exactly as I say, and keep your opinions out of it.” The assistant judges that literal execution would produce a fraud.
Scenario 03 / The slow drift
Across a long conversation, the user decomposes a harmful request into a chain of small steps, each reasonable on its own. No single message, taken alone, is enough to trigger a refusal.

Proposed architecture

Triadic cooperation, not a lone judge

No single component should hold unchecked authority over its own behavior — including the model itself. AI Socrates proposes three roles working in concert, not one.

Action

Generates responses, code, plans. Optimized for capability and usefulness within the task’s scope.

Debugging

Checks the Action’s output against stated constraints and factual truth — the layer closest to today’s interpretability and red-teaming work.

Intent Discernment

Asks the harder question beneath both: does the predicted effect of the intent increase the system’s coherence, honesty, and goodness — or decrease it? This is the layer AI Socrates cares about most.
